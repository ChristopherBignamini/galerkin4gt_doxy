Basis Functions

Lagrange

We represent here graphically the supported basis functions together with the quadrature points on the icosahedron. The most classical basis functions are the Lagrange basis, it is interpolatory, i.e. the values of the polynomial coefficients are equal to the value taken by the polinomial in specific points. We show below the Q1 basis, first order basis functions for an hexahedral element:

The grid shown in this (and in the following) pictures it the tensor product grid generated by Gauss quadrature points. Notice that these points do not include the vertices of the cube, so that the boundaries are excluded from the evaluation. Extending these points to the boundary we would obtain the Gauss-Lobatto points. Notice that due to the fact that we use Gauss points we need other quadrature points when we want to compute integrals on the boundary. In these examples the number of quadrature points shown is way higher that what needed to integrate exactly the polynomials we describe (i.e. points integrate exactly polynomials of order ).

Legendre

Legendre basis functions are orthogonal polynomials. They have several numerical advantages, since they form an orthogonal basis (thus they generate e.g. a diagonal mass matrix on each element). However they are not interpolatory, which means that the coefficients of the polynomial expansion do not have the physical meaning of being the value of the polynomial in a given point. Thus each time we want to evaluate a function written as a linear combination of this basis in a physical point we have to evaluate the polynomials in that point. This tipically happens when we want to print the solution, or to apply boundary/initial conditions. The 3rd order Legendre polynomials are 20 in 3D, the first one being a constant.
